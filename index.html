The reason we picked these dates

Gion Matsuri — Japan's biggest festival

Our five nights in Kyoto land right on the peak of a festival that has run since the year 869. Giant lantern-lit floats built without a single nail, streets closed to traffic and filled with food stalls, and a huge parade of 12-ton floats hand-pulled through the city. This is the heart of the trip.

Kyoto · July 14 – 17

Gion Matsuri 🏮

Kyoto's biggest festival has run for over 1,150 years — it began in 869 as a plea to end a plague — and it fills all of July. We're there for the four peak days: three "eve" nights of lantern-lit floats and street food, then the giant morning parade. Nothing else on the trip is like it.

The Parade
9:00–13:00 — 12-ton floats hand-pulled 3 km
Built without nails. The 23 yamaboko floats are assembled in the streets each July from wood and rope alone — some stand 25 m tall. Watching crews lash them together is a spectacle of its own.
Chimaki charms. Each float sells bamboo-grass amulets that protect your home for a year. Kyoto locals hang them over the door — ours flies back to Seattle.
Gion-bayashi. The "kon-chiki-chin" of flutes, taiko and bells rings from the floats all three nights — it's the sound Kyoto waits all year for.
Our hotel is inside the festival zone. Kawaramachi is steps from the float streets and the parade route — when the girls get tired, bed is a 5-minute walk.

The heat plan

Mid-July is real summer. Our rhythm: out by 8–9 AM, indoor or AC stop over midday, back out after 4 PM — which is exactly when the festival comes alive. Cooling towels, folding fans, and a vending-machine drink every hour (they're on every corner for a reason).
